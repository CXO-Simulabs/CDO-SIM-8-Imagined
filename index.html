What You'll Face

You are the newly appointed CDO of a mid-size consumer goods company. The company has 12 million customer transaction records spanning 8 years, supply chain data, and product performance data — but none of it has ever been formally valued, catalogued, or treated as a strategic asset. The CEO is curious about data's potential. The CFO thinks data valuation is speculative. Your mission: build the information asset register, establish a valuation methodology, design a monetisation roadmap, and shift the culture from treating data as an IT cost to managing it as a strategic asset.

Data Asset Valuation & Infonomics

Framework 1 of 4
The Three Types of Data Value
Laney's framework for measuring what most companies ignore

Doug Laney, former VP of Research at Gartner, coined the term infonomics — the economics of information. His core observation: most corporations exhibit greater discipline in tracking, managing, and reporting on their office furniture than on their data assets.

Laney identifies three distinct types of economic value in a data asset:

  • Intrinsic value: What is the data worth in its own right? Measured by completeness, accuracy, uniqueness, and timeliness. A dataset of 12 million verified customer records spanning 8 years has high intrinsic value because of its depth and historical range.
  • Business value: What decisions does the data enable, and what is the economic value of those decisions? Customer data that drives pricing, segmentation, and product development has enormous business value even if the data itself cost little to collect.
  • Performance value: How does the data improve the performance of processes, products, and people? Supply chain data that reduces defect rates from 12% to 4% has measurable performance value.
CDO Insight: The goal is directional credibility, not accounting exactitude. You do not need to value data to the penny. You need to show the board that data has measurable economic value across three dimensions — and that the current level of investment in data management is inadequate relative to that value.

The Information Asset Register
The data equivalent of a fixed asset register

Every physical asset in a company has an asset register entry — documenting ownership, location, condition, depreciation, and value. Every desk. Every laptop. Every company vehicle.

Almost no data asset does. The CDO's job is to build and maintain an information asset register — an inventory of the organisation's data assets with attributes including:

  • Provenance: Where does the data come from? How is it collected?
  • Quality: How complete, accurate, and timely is it?
  • Ownership: Who is accountable for this data asset?
  • Usage: Which business processes and decisions depend on it?
  • Estimated value: What is the data worth across the three value dimensions?
CDO Insight: Start with the top 20 data assets ranked by business value — do not attempt to catalogue everything. A fixed asset register does not start with every paperclip; it starts with the material assets. Your information asset register should do the same. Build incrementally, prove value, then expand.

Eight Monetisation Pathways
From internal decision improvement to external data licensing

Laney documents eight distinct pathways through which organisations can monetise data. These range from implicit monetisation (using data to improve internal decisions, reduce costs, and optimise operations) to explicit monetisation (selling, licensing, or trading data with third parties).

The critical sequencing insight:

  • Year 1 — Implicit monetisation: Use data to improve internal decisions. This is low-risk, generates measurable value quickly, and builds the governance infrastructure needed for external monetisation.
  • Year 2 — Controlled external sharing: Data partnerships, mutual benefit arrangements with supply chain partners, industry consortia.
  • Year 3 — Explicit monetisation: Selling or licensing data to third parties, building data products, creating new revenue streams from data assets.
CDO Insight: The most common mistake is jumping to explicit monetisation (selling data) before building the governance infrastructure to do it responsibly. Without anonymisation validation, consent verification, and regulatory review, external data monetisation is a liability, not an asset. Sequence matters: internal first, external when ready.

Framework 4 of 4
Making the Board Business Case
Three types of evidence the sceptical CFO needs to see

The CFO's objection is predictable: "Data valuation is too speculative to put in board reporting." Three types of public evidence support the infonomics case:

  • Acquisition premiums: When Microsoft acquired LinkedIn for $26.2 billion in 2016, the tangible assets were a fraction of the price. The premium was the market's implicit valuation of 400 million professional profiles. Data is already valued in M&A — it is just not valued in management reporting.
  • Regulatory recognition: GDPR and CCPA treat personal data as valuable enough to require explicit consent and significant penalties for misuse. Regulators have implicitly recognised that data is an asset worth protecting.
  • Market capitalisation correlation: Academic research documents a positive correlation between data maturity and market capitalisation. Companies with sophisticated data governance earn valuation premiums over less-governed competitors.
CDO Insight: Position data valuation as a strategic management tool, separate from statutory reporting. The CFO cannot object to management reporting the way they can object to balance sheet inclusion. You are not asking the auditors to sign off — you are showing the board that their most valuable assets are invisible in current reporting, and that this invisibility leads to systematic underinvestment.
